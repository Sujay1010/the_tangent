Why nobody actually teaches you the good stuff
12 min read CS Theory June 2026

There's a moment that happens in almost every university lecture. The professor is mid-sentence, explaining something by the book, and then — just for a second — they hesitate. You can see it. Their eyes go slightly off-script. They're about to say something that isn't in the slides.

And then they don't. They pull back. They return to the PowerPoint. The moment passes.

I've started calling this the Retreat. And I think the Retreat is responsible for more confusion among students than any bad explanation ever could be.

What lives between the slides

The interesting part of any subject is almost never in the formal curriculum. It lives in the gaps — the reason a concept was invented, the failed attempts before the clean solution, the edge cases that break the beautiful theory.

Take gradient descent. The textbook version is clean. Loss function, partial derivatives, step size, repeat. Elegant. Tidy. Completely useless for understanding why it actually works in practice.

The textbook teaches you to drive in a parking lot. The interesting stuff is what happens when you hit an actual road.

Why professors retreat

It's not because they don't know. Most professors know far more than they teach. The retreat happens because the curriculum has a shape, and going off-script risks losing people, running over time, or opening a question they can't close in 50 minutes.

What you do about it

You go looking for it yourself. Read the papers the textbook is based on — not for the math, but for the motivation sections. Talk to your professor after class about the thing they almost said. Find the people who go on tangents. That's what this site is.
