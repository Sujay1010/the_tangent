11 min read Mathematics April 2026

Hi. Back in college, have you ever wondered what those creepy, disturbing concepts in your math book labelled 'Transforms' actually meant? If you did, good for you because I never did and that ended up bad for me. Yes, I am talking about Fourier, Laplace and Z Transforms. The damn things which cost me a big chunk of grades in my first two years of college. But it won't again (I hope so).

Fourier and his hot curves

When my profs were taking transforms, I just couldn't understand why? I thought that Jean-Baptiste Fourier just woke up one day and went, “You know what the world needs? Integrals with imaginary numbers" (I still think so, but the concept is cool). In reality, he solved a real-world problem. To solve the dispersion of heat through an object (when stopped heating, hot part cools, cold part warms. That's dispersion of heat). To predict temperature at every point over time. Prolly they didn't have thermometers back then, idk bro. The thermal curves look ugly (like yo moth-Ahem!). He saw it could be broken into simple waves. The practical upshot

Next time you're very confident about something, ask yourself what evidence would actually change your mind. If the answer is nothing — that's not confidence. That's a prior so strong that no evidence could move it. Which, statistically speaking, is a problem.
