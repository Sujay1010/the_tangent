The Siblings - Fourier, Laplace and Z
5 min read Mathematics April 2026

Hi. Back in college, have you ever wondered what those creepy, disturbing concepts in your math book labelled 'Transforms' actually meant? If you did, good for you because I never did and that ended up bad for me. Yes, I am talking about Fourier, Laplace and Z Transforms. The damn things which cost me a big chunk of grades in my first two years of college. But it won't again (I hope so).

Fourier and his hot curves

When my profs were taking transforms, I just couldn't understand why? I thought that Jean-Baptiste Fourier just woke up one day and went, “You know what the world needs? Integrals with imaginary numbers" (I still think so, but the concept is cool). In reality, he solved a real-world problem. To solve the dispersion of heat through an object (when stopped heating, hot part cools, cold part warms. That's dispersion of heat). To predict temperature at every point over time. Prolly they didn't have thermometers back then, idk bro. The thermal curves look ugly (like yo moth-Ahem!). He saw it could be broken into simple waves. And suddenly every curve he saw (no! not those.) could be broken into simple waves. Sine waves. And Fourier could actually distinguish different frequency components like take an ugly wave and he would use the expression X(ω)=∫x(t)e^(−jωt)dt to deduce it to something like 2*sin(10t)+6.7888*sin(69t), which gives you the frequency components. Cool ryt?

Laplace, the drama detector

Let me tell you something more cooler. Take the curve as an exponential function. x(t)=e^(-t) substituted in fourier expression makes the integral blows up, diverges to infinity. Now, we know fourier cannot distinguish between sin(2π10t) and e^2t*sin(2π10t), it treats both as same 10Hz and cannot say if its growing or decaying. Laplace solves it. With s=σ+jω. What happens? The expression ∫x(t)e^(−st)dt multiplies the diverging integral with a decaying exponential and then comes the Fourier. Easy now? With the transform you get poles (unlike Max Verstappen 2026). Poles are the values of 's' that make the denominator zero and the whole expression goes to infinity. If those poles sit in the left half of the complex plane, congratulations, your system calms down over time. If a pole drifts into the right half, your system will diverge forever.

Z, The one with the digital fetish

Laplace works on continuous signals x(t). The moment you sample x(t) into discrete points x[n] (which is what every computer/digital system does), if you try to just plug that into the Laplace integral, you get a sum of impulses, and the exponential term becomes e^(−snT), i.e.(e^{-sT})^n. Here's the problem: e^{-sT} is periodic, so it repeats every j*(2π/T) along the imaginary axis. So the entire s-plane is just the same information copy-pasted infinitely, over and over, in horizontal strips. That's redundant and awkward you're carrying infinite copies of identical data just to describe one discrete sequence. Confusing? Let's just say Laplace rizzes with analogs but fumbles with discrete baddies. His brother Z ain't no fumbler. He rizzes up discrete baddies by defining z=e^{sT} directly, which collapses all those repeating strips into one single complex plane which in turn makes the imaginary axis, an unit circle. Z isn't a new idea, it's Laplace modified to remove the periodicity that sampling introduces.
