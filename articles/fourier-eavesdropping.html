The Fourier transform is just eavesdropping on signals
4 min read Math May 2026

The Fourier transform takes a signal — any signal — and tells you which frequencies it's made of. You give it a complicated messy wave and it hands back a clean list of sine waves that add up to that mess.

Which means every signal is secretly a combination of pure tones. Your voice. An earthquake. The electrical activity in your brain. All of it — just sine waves pretending to be something more complicated.

The Fourier transform is just listening very carefully and going: ah yes, I can hear that you're actually 47 different frequencies wearing a trench coat.

What I find genuinely wild is that this works. That reality is organised in a way where decomposing things into sine waves is useful. That's not obvious. That's a fact about the universe that we're lucky to have stumbled into.
