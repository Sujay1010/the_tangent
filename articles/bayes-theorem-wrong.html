Bayes' theorem and why you're always wrong about being right
11 min read Statistics April 2026

Bayes' theorem is one of those things that sounds simple, looks like a formula, and then quietly dismantles your entire understanding of how confidence works.

The short version: your belief about something should update based on new evidence. The degree of update depends on how likely that evidence was under your original belief versus the alternative.

Why this is deeply uncomfortable

Because it means your current beliefs are just your prior beliefs plus the evidence you've encountered so far. Two people with different starting points can look at identical evidence and rationally arrive at different conclusions. This isn't relativism. It's math.

Confidence isn't a measure of truth. It's a measure of how much your evidence has shifted your prior.

The practical upshot

Next time you're very confident about something, ask yourself what evidence would actually change your mind. If the answer is nothing — that's not confidence. That's a prior so strong that no evidence could move it. Which, statistically speaking, is a problem.
