Why nobody actually teaches you the good stuff
12 min read CS Theory June 19, 2026

There's a moment that happens in almost every university lecture. The professor is mid-sentence, explaining something by the book, and then — just for a second — they hesitate. You can see it. Their eyes go slightly off-script. They're about to say something that isn't in the slides.

And then they don't. They pull back. They return to the PowerPoint. The moment passes.

I've started calling this the Retreat. And I think the Retreat is responsible for more confusion among students than any bad explanation ever could be.

What actually lives between the slides

Here's the thing nobody tells you: the interesting part of any subject is almost never in the formal curriculum. It lives in the gaps. It's in the reason a concept was invented in the first place, the failed attempts before the clean solution, the edge cases that break the beautiful theory.

Take gradient descent — the backbone of pretty much every machine learning model you've heard of. The textbook version is clean. Loss function, partial derivatives, step size, repeat. Elegant. Tidy. Completely useless for understanding why it actually works in practice.

The textbook teaches you to drive in a parking lot. The interesting stuff is what happens when you hit an actual road.

Why professors retreat

It's not because they don't know. Most professors know far more than they teach — they've spent decades in the weeds of their field. The retreat happens because the curriculum has a shape, and going off-script risks losing people, running over time, or opening a question they can't close in 50 minutes.

The incentive structure of education isn't set up to reward tangents. It rewards coverage. So the interesting stuff — the real texture of a subject — gets quietly left out.

So what do you do about it

You go looking for it yourself. That's genuinely the only answer. Read the papers the textbook is based on — not for the math, but for the motivation sections, where the authors explain why they were trying to solve this in the first place. Those sections are gold.

Talk to your professor after class, not about the assignment — about the thing they almost said. They'll usually tell you. They wanted to say it anyway.

And find people who go on tangents. Find the blogs, the YouTube channels, the Twitter accounts, the random Substack writers who can't help but follow a thread wherever it goes. They're the ones who actually make subjects make sense.

That's what this site is, by the way. A collection of tangents that couldn't stay in the margins.
